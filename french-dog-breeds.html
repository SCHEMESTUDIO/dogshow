French Dog Breeds: 12 to Know

France has produced some of the world's most recognizable companion and working breeds — from the couch-loving French Bulldog to the mountain-guarding Great Pyrenees. Here's an honest rundown of what each one actually needs from an owner.

Quick answer: France's best-known breeds include the French Bulldog, Bichon Frise, Papillon, Brittany, Beauceron, Berger Picard, Dogue de Bordeaux, Braque Français, Basset Fauve de Bretagne, and Great Pyrenees — ranging from tiny apartment companions to giant livestock guardians.

French Bulldog

The one everyone already knows, and for good reason. Compact, snub-nosed, bat-eared, built for city apartments rather than long runs. Developed in the 1800s from English Bulldog stock brought over by Nottingham lacemakers who settled in France, then refined into something distinctly Parisian. Even-tempered and sociable, the Frenchie does best with owners who can manage its brachycephalic build thoughtfully — see the AKC's own breed standard for specifics. Full profile: /breeds/french-bulldog.

Bichon Frise

A small, cheerful, curly-coated companion breed whose name literally means "curly lap dog." Descended from Mediterranean water dogs and refined in French and Italian courts, the Bichon has centuries of practice being adored rather than working for a living. Good-natured, alert, and reliably cheerful — a dog that treats every guest like an old friend. More at /breeds/bichon-frise.

Papillon

Named for its butterfly-shaped ears, the Papillon is a toy spaniel with a surprisingly athletic streak for its size. It shows up in French court portraiture going back centuries, always perched somewhere aristocratic. Bright, quick to learn, and more energetic than its dainty looks suggest. See /breeds/papillon.

Brittany

A pointing spaniel from the Brittany region, bred for covering ground efficiently in the field. Compact, orange-and-white or liver-and-white, and built for a working day rather than a sofa. Friendly and eager, but genuinely needs exercise and a job — a Brittany without a purpose gets inventive in ways owners rarely enjoy. Details at /breeds/brittany.

Beauceron

A large, imposing French herding breed with a history dating back centuries in the plains around Beauce. Confident, protective, and loyal to its family, the Beauceron is a serious dog for a serious owner — this isn't a first-time-dog-owner breed, but it's a superb one for someone experienced with working breeds. More at /breeds/beauceron.

Berger Picard

France's oldest herding breed by most accounts, distinctive for its rough, weatherproof coat and slightly comic, expressive face. Rare outside France even now, and rare in North America generally. Independent-minded, loyal, and better suited to an active household than a quiet one. Full profile: /breeds/berger-picard.

Dogue de Bordeaux

A massive, wrinkled mastiff-type breed from the Bordeaux region, historically used for guarding and hauling. Gentle and devoted with family, imposing to strangers, and — per the AKC standard — a breed that needs a confident, experienced handler given its size and strength. See /breeds/dogue-de-bordeaux.

Braque Français

A pointing breed with two recognized varieties (Gascogne and Pyrénées types), among the ancestors of many modern pointers across Europe. Affectionate, biddable, and built for long days afield. Less common as a household pet in the US, but worth knowing as one of the breeds behind half the continent's gundog lines. More at /breeds/braque-francais.

Basset Fauve de Bretagne

The lesser-known cousin of the familiar Basset Hound — a French scent hound bred for hunting in dense Breton undergrowth, with a wiry reddish-fawn coat and considerably more get-up-and-go than the droopier Basset Hound most people picture. If you're drawn to the low-slung, long-eared silhouette but want a dog with real working drive, this is the one to research. See /breeds/basset-fauve-de-bretagne.

Great Pyrenees

The FCI credits France as the breed's country of origin — the FCI standard for the Pyrenean Mountain Dog lists its origin as France, and the breed originates from the eastern or French side of the Pyrenees Mountains, which separate France and Spain. Its true home, though, is the mountain range itself, and the closely related Pyrenean Mastiff on the Spanish side shows how tangled the border history really is — it is recognised as a separate breed from the Mastín del Pirineo or Pyrenean Mastiff from the Spanish side of the mountains, but they are closely related. Bred for centuries to guard flocks against wolves and bears, it's a calm, watchful, enormous dog — patient with family, wary of strangers, and happiest with space to patrol. Full profile: /breeds/great-pyrenees.
